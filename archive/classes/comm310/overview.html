Class Overview

As described in Brother Judkin's Syllabus, the purpose of this class is to teach students the basics of web design. We'll become familiar with a few different languages, including HTML, CSS, and PHP. Also included will be discussions of SEO, usabilty, and the importance of following web standards.

As with all classes, the level of understanding and mastery students achieve will depend on the time and effort they are willing to give. Those who wish to become future web designers or developers will be able delve in as deep as they would like into their assignments and projects, and those who are just seeking to become acquainted with the world of online media creation will able to do so and be successful as well. I would encourage everyone to learn concepts as fully as they are able because online media is certainly becoming more and more prevalent, and so the ability to produce it will become an extremely valuable and marketable skill.

Class Flow/Structure

As of Winter 2011, the class is three days a week, twice as normal class/lecture time, and then once week as a lab. Participation plays a fairly large roll in class, so its is important to do as the Learning Model suggests, come prepared to class to teach other students, and look for opportunities to share what you learn out of class. Refer to the online schedule to keep track of reading assignments, projects, and topics for lab days.

Most assignments and quizzes are open book and open web, which is very much a real-world condition, because students' ability to understand principles and then comfortably use resources online is more important than just memorizing code. In real world situations, web designers will always have such resources available and will use them often. Memorization certainly helps and will come with practice, but by no means is memorization a requirement to be a successful web designer.

Materials Needed

The hard copy resource required (as of Winter 2010) is the HTML Dog book called: "The Best Practice Guide to XHTML & CSS. This book teaches concepts very simply and clearly, but just as often I would recommend refering to online resources when looking up code,because the HTML Dog book is becoming out of date. HTML 5 will very soon be the most current widely supported version of HTML, and CSS will soon complete its transition to version 3.

It is also helpful to have some sort of code editor, such as Dreamweaver(which is super expensive), or NetBeans,TextWrangler(for Mac), or Notepad++(Windows). The later three are all free and relatively easy to use.

Keep Skills Polished

The web is a rapidly evolving world, so hopefully in this class students will learn how to use available resources to stay educated and up to date. A great way to do this is to ocassionaly visit web design blogs and other resources online. Some great sites for this are listed on Brother Judkin's website.
